ABOUT THE TEXAS TRIBUNE

The Texas Tribune is a nonprofit, nonpartisan digital news organization focused on Texas politics and policy. Founded in 2009, it has become one of the most respected state-level news organizations in the country. They're fully digital — no print edition — and they've been a pioneer in using technology to serve journalism.

Their data visuals team is led by Chris Essig, who has been a newsroom developer for about a decade. The team builds charts, maintains public databases, and analyzes data to help reporters hold elected officials accountable. The team includes developers like Carla Astudillo (Senior Data Visuals Developer), Yuriko Schumacher (designer/developer), Edison Wu, Dan Keemahill (data reporter), and Andrew (developer).

THE TECHNOLOGY STACK

The Tribune's stack spans several technologies:

1. Django (Python) — Their backend framework. Django powers their CMS, data apps, and internal tools. The Tribune has been a Django shop for years, which is common among news organizations. Django's admin panel is especially valuable — editors and reporters can manage content without engineering help.

2. D3.js — Their primary data visualization library. Used for interactive charts, election maps, data explorers, demographic breakdowns, and all the custom graphics that can't be built with off-the-shelf tools.

3. React — Used alongside D3 for more complex interactive features and page-level applications.

4. Node.js — Used in their build tooling and scaffolding systems.

5. Nunjucks — A templating language (similar to Jinja2) used in their graphic-building workflow for pulling in text and data from Google Spreadsheets.

6. SCSS/Sass — Their CSS preprocessor of choice, using the dart-sass implementation.

7. Webpack — JavaScript bundler used in their data-visuals-create tool with a modern/legacy bundle approach for browser compatibility.

8. AWS S3 — Their deployment target for graphics and features, with assets served from S3.

9. Google Sheets + Google Docs — Integrated into their build pipeline. Reporters can put data in Google Sheets, and the build system pulls it directly into the graphic. Google Docs can be formatted with ArchieML (a structured text format invented at the NYT) for longer narrative pieces.

10. PostgreSQL — The likely database backend (standard for Django projects, especially at news organizations that deal with large datasets).

OPEN SOURCE PROJECTS ON GITHUB

The Tribune has 224 repositories on GitHub at github.com/texastribune. Key ones:

1. data-visuals-create — Their main scaffolding tool for creating data visualization projects. Generates project boilerplates with Nunjucks templating, SCSS styles, Webpack-bundled JavaScript, Google Sheets data integration, and S3 deployment. This is the foundation of their graphics workflow.

2. data-visuals-guides — Documentation and guides for the data visuals team. Shows their conventions, style guides, and workflows.

3. newsapps-graphic-kit — An older boilerplate for embeddable graphics, built with Gulp, BrowserSync, Nunjucks, and NPR's pym.js for responsive iframes in CMS environments. Framework-agnostic — developers add their own visualization libraries (D3, etc.) as needed.

4. newsapps-app-kit — For building special features and series that live outside the regular CMS. Full standalone pages with custom layouts.

5. generator-tt-newsapps — A Yeoman generator that scaffolds out front-end web apps or embeddable graphics using Texas Tribune stylings.

6. scuole — A Python project (MIT licensed) for education data, with scuole-data containing the datasets. This is likely behind their school ratings and education data tools.

7. queso-ui — Their shared UI library of styles and icons (MIT licensed). "Queso" — very Texas.

8. docks — Their Docker library for standardized development environments.

9. django-locking — A Django package that prevents concurrent editing in the admin interface. Useful when multiple editors are working in the CMS simultaneously.

WHAT THE DATA VISUALS TEAM BUILDS

Based on their 2024 and 2025 year-end retrospectives:

2024 highlights:
- Election results pages for the March primary and November general election — the most comprehensive election results pages in Texas. Built by Carla Astudillo. These pages include county-level maps, race-by-race results, and a personalization feature where readers can enter their address to see their specific races.
- An interactive visualization showing how only a fraction of Texas voters decide elections — built by Yuriko Schumacher, using a "small fish in a big pond" visual metaphor.
- Border wall progress tracking — Yuriko examined how much of the proposed border wall has been built, finding billions spent over three years with the barrier still far short of goals.
- Housing crisis analysis — Carla gathered and analyzed zoning data from 11 cities to show how dominant single-family housing is across Texas cities. Described as "the most visually ambitious" piece of the year.
- Visualization of Dan Keemahill showing sharp increases in Republican support in border counties over time.
- Extreme weather visualizations — flooding scope in East Texas, comprehensive weather extremes story.

2025 highlights:
- Congressional redistricting tool — interactive tool for exploring proposed district boundaries
- Legislative session tracker — tracked key proposals and let readers see how their local representatives voted on consequential measures
- Water crisis explainer and interactive lookup tool — readers could see how severe water shortages are expected to be in their area
- July 4 floods coverage — Edison Wu illustrated camps along the Guadalupe River near flooding areas and showed how historically extreme the flooding was. 135+ people died.
- Measles outbreak tracker — Dan Keemahill analyzed vaccination trends in Texas schools
- Education data releases — A-F school ratings (first time in 5 years), higher education outcomes data

THE ELECTION RESULTS PIPELINE

This is the most technically interesting workflow at the Tribune and worth understanding:

1. Data comes from the Associated Press (AP), which gathers voting information from the Secretary of State's office, county election sites, and stringers on the ground.
2. A Django management command (most likely) polls the AP results feed on a schedule (every few minutes on election night).
3. Results are saved to the database via Django's ORM.
4. The frontend election results pages (hosted at apps.texastribune.org) fetch data from the API.
5. D3 (or another visualization library) renders interactive maps and results tables.
6. As new data arrives, the pipeline updates automatically — the enter/update pattern in D3 handles new and changed results gracefully.
7. The pages include a personalization feature where users can enter their zip code to see relevant races.

This pipeline is a perfect example of Django and D3 working together: Django handles data ingestion and serving, D3 handles visualization.

THE APPS SUBDOMAIN

The Tribune publishes major interactives at apps.texastribune.org — these are standalone features built outside the main CMS. Examples:
- apps.texastribune.org/features/2024/texas-2024-general-election-results/
- apps.texastribune.org/features/2024/primary-election-results-2024/
- apps.texastribune.org/features/2024/texas-may-2024-runoff-election-results/

These are deployed to AWS S3 and are separate from the main texastribune.org site (which runs on WordPress/Newspack for editorial content). The separation lets the data visuals team ship custom code without being constrained by the CMS.

WHY THE TRIBUNE'S APPROACH MATTERS

1. Open source culture — They share their tools publicly. This is rare and shows they value the broader journalism-technology community.

2. Two-system architecture — WordPress/Newspack for daily editorial content, custom Django + standalone apps for data-heavy features. This is a practical, modern approach.

3. Data-to-story pipeline — Reporters find stories in data (using Python, SQL, spreadsheets). Developers visualize those stories with D3. The admin panel lets editors manage and update data. Management commands automate data ingestion. It's a complete loop.

4. Team structure — They have dedicated data visuals developers who can code AND understand journalism. The job listings mention both technical skills and editorial judgment.

5. The team contributed to roughly 100 impactful stories in 2025 alone. That's a story roughly every 3-4 days. This is a high-output, high-impact team.
