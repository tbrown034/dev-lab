HOW TO FRAME THE DJANGO GAP

Rule number one: don't apologize for it. Don't lead with it. Don't bring it up unless they ask. And when they do ask, here's the framework:

Step 1 — Acknowledge it honestly but briefly: "I haven't worked in Django professionally."

Step 2 — Show conceptual understanding: "But I understand the framework — models, views, URL routing, the ORM, the admin panel, and how Django REST Framework serves JSON APIs. I've been studying the Tribune's stack specifically."

Step 3 — Pivot to transferable experience: "I've built full-stack applications with Next.js and PostgreSQL. The patterns are the same — routing, database queries, API endpoints, authentication. Django just has different names for the same concepts."

Step 4 — Demonstrate learning velocity: "I taught myself React, TypeScript, and full-stack development in under two years while working full-time as an investigative journalist. I learn fast because I have a journalist's research instincts."

Step 5 — Deliver the real pitch: "My Django learning curve is weeks, not months. My 15 years of editorial judgment and investigative skills — that's what takes a career to build and can't be learned from a tutorial."

That last line is the key. It reframes the conversation from "what Trevor lacks" to "what Trevor has that nobody else does." The Django gap is temporary. The journalism background is permanent.

HOW TO FRAME THE D3 GAP

Same approach — don't apologize, demonstrate understanding, pivot to what matters.

Show you understand D3's mental model: "I understand that D3 is a data binding toolkit, not a charting library. You bind data to DOM elements, use scales to convert data values to visual properties, and use generators for paths and projections for maps."

Show you've done related work: "I've built charts with Chart.js and experimented with D3. The concepts — scales, axes, data binding, SVG manipulation — I understand. My learning curve is the D3 API specifics, not the visualization concepts."

Then pivot to the thing that actually matters for the Tribune's work: "What I bring that most D3 developers don't is the editorial eye. I know which data point is the story. I know what a reader needs to understand versus what's just noise. That editorial judgment shapes every visualization decision — what to highlight, what to simplify, what to leave out entirely. You can teach someone the D3 API in a few weeks. You can't teach them 15 years of newsroom instincts."

KILLER TALKING POINTS — MEMORIZE THESE

These are Trevor's go-to lines. They should feel natural, not rehearsed, but he should know them cold.

The opening pitch: "What drew me to this role is that it sits at the exact intersection of my two careers — investigative journalism and web development. I've spent 15 years finding stories in data. Now I want to build the tools that help readers see those stories."

On the Tribune specifically: "I've been following the Tribune's work for years as a journalist. Your election results pages, the schools explorer, the housing zoning analysis — this is the kind of work that makes me excited to be in this field."

On the team's engineering culture: "I've looked at the team's GitHub repos — data-visuals-create for scaffolding, queso-ui for shared styles, scuole for education data. The investment in internal tooling tells me this is a team that takes craft seriously."

On AI (it's in the job listing): "The listing mentions AI experimentation, and that's an area I'm actively working in. I built an AI news aggregator and an AI model comparison tool. I think there's huge potential for AI to help with data analysis, story discovery, and making graphics more accessible."

The closing statement: "I know my Django and D3 experience is growing rather than deep. But I also know that the hardest skills for this role — editorial judgment, investigative instincts, the ability to communicate complex data to readers — those are skills I've been building for 15 years. The technical gaps are a matter of weeks. The journalism background is a matter of a career."

SMART QUESTIONS TO ASK THEM

Good questions show preparation and genuine curiosity. Trevor should pick 3-4 of these depending on how the conversation flows:

About workflow: "What does the workflow look like between the data visuals team and reporters? How early does the team get involved in a story?"

About technical decisions: "How does the team decide when something needs a custom D3 graphic versus an off-the-shelf tool like Datawrapper?"

About election night (the team's signature product): "What's the deployment pipeline for election night? How does the team handle last-minute data issues?"

About AI: "The listing mentions AI experimentation — what areas is the team most excited about?"

About onboarding: "What would the first few months look like for someone in this role? What would I be working on first?"

About team culture: "How does the team handle code review and technical standards? I noticed you have data-visuals-guides on GitHub."

About challenges: "What's the biggest technical challenge the team is facing right now?"

Important: Do NOT ask about salary, benefits, or time off in the first interview. Those conversations come later.

HANDLING THE TOUGH QUESTIONS

These are the moments that make or break an interview. Trevor should have answers ready so he doesn't freeze.

"Tell me about a time you used D3."
Answer: "I've studied D3 extensively and built experimental visualizations. But more importantly, I understand the mental model and I've used Chart.js in production. I learn new libraries quickly — I went from zero JavaScript to building full-stack React apps in under a year."

"Do you have Django experience?"
Answer: "Not professionally, but I understand the framework deeply — models and the ORM for data, views and URL routing for logic, templates for rendering, and Django REST Framework for APIs. My Python is solid and I've built production apps with PostgreSQL, which is the standard Django database."

"Why are you leaving journalism?"
Answer: "I'm not leaving journalism — I'm evolving how I practice it. I've spent 15 years finding stories in data with spreadsheets and documents. Now I want to build the tools that help millions of readers understand those stories visually."

"What's your biggest weakness?"
Answer: Frame it honestly but forward-looking: "My Django and D3 are self-taught and growing. But I compensate by being a relentless learner and by bringing something most candidates can't — the editorial judgment that comes from 15 years in a newsroom."

BODY LANGUAGE AND ENERGY

This isn't just about what Trevor says — it's about how he shows up.

Be genuinely enthusiastic. The Tribune does incredible work. Trevor's excitement about their projects should be real, not performed. Enthusiasm is contagious and memorable.

Don't be defensive about gaps. When Django or D3 come up, be matter-of-fact. State what you know, state what you're learning, pivot to strengths. Defensive energy makes gaps feel bigger than they are. Confident energy makes them feel manageable.

Ask follow-up questions throughout the conversation. Don't save all questions for the end. When they describe a project or a workflow, ask a genuine follow-up. This shows curiosity and engagement — both qualities they want in a teammate.

Take notes during the interview. Bring a notebook or have a notes app ready. It shows you're engaged and taking the conversation seriously. It also gives you something to do with your hands if you're nervous.

Mention specific Tribune projects by name. Nothing impresses an interviewer more than a candidate who has clearly done their homework. Reference the election results pages, the housing zoning analysis, the schools explorer, the water crisis tool. Show them you've been paying attention.
