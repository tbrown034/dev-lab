THE TEAM'S OUTPUT — 2024-2025 HIGHLIGHTS

The data visuals team contributed to roughly 100 stories per year. That's a story every 3-4 days. This is a high-output, high-impact team. Here are the standout projects that define what they do and how they think:

ELECTION RESULTS PAGES (2024)

This is the flagship project — and almost certainly the first major system Trevor would inherit.

- Built primarily by Carla Astudillo (the person whose role Trevor is interviewing for)
- Covered the March primary, May runoff, and November general election
- Widely regarded as the most comprehensive election results pages in Texas
- Features: county-level interactive maps, race-by-race results tables, personalized "find your races" search by address
- Tech stack in action: Django backend ingesting AP election data, Django REST Framework API serving JSON, D3 rendering maps and charts, React for interactive components
- URL pattern: apps.texastribune.org/features/2024/texas-2024-general-election-results/
- Why it matters for Trevor: If he gets this job, he'll likely own this system for the 2026 midterms. He needs to understand the full pipeline — the Django management commands that poll AP data, the ORM models that store results, the DRF serializers that shape the JSON, and the D3 code that renders county-level maps with geoPath and choropleth color scales.

THE VOTER FISH-IN-A-POND VISUALIZATION (2024)

- Built by Yuriko Schumacher
- Used a "small fish in a big pond" visual metaphor to show how only a fraction of Texas voters actually decide elections
- This is a creative, non-standard visualization — the kind of thing D3 excels at because you can't do this with Chart.js or a charting library with pre-built chart types
- Shows that the team values creative storytelling, not just standard bar charts
- D3's power is custom mark rendering — when the story needs a unique visual form, D3 can build it from primitives (circles, paths, force simulations, whatever the metaphor demands)

HOUSING ZONING ANALYSIS (2024)

- Built by Carla Astudillo

- The workflow: acquire data (likely through FOIA and direct requests to city planning departments) -> clean and analyze -> visualize -> tell the story
- This is the kind of project where Trevor's investigative journalism skills (records requests, data validation, source verification) become a superpower

PATTERNS IN THEIR WORK — WHAT THE TEAM VALUES

Looking across all these projects, five patterns emerge that define how the Texas Tribune data visuals team thinks:

1. PERSONALIZATION
Almost every major project lets readers enter their address or location to see personally relevant data. The election results pages have "find your races." The water crisis tool has "see your area." The schools explorer has "look up your school." The redistricting tool has "see your district." The team doesn't just publish Texas-wide data — they make it about YOUR school, YOUR district, YOUR water supply, YOUR representative. This is a design philosophy, not just a feature. It's rooted in the journalistic principle that stories matter most when readers see themselves in them.

2. DATA ACQUISITION AS JOURNALISM
Many projects involve gathering data that isn't readily available — FOIA requests, scraping state agencies, combining multiple datasets from different sources. The housing zoning analysis required collecting data from 11 different city planning departments. The measles tracker required getting vaccination records from schools. The team doesn't just visualize data that falls into their lap — they go get it, like investigative reporters working a story.

3. CREATIVE VISUALIZATION
The fish-in-a-pond voter metaphor, the housing zoning maps, the border wall geographic tracker — this team doesn't default to bar charts. They find the visual form that tells the story best. When a bar chart is the right answer, they use a bar chart. But when the story calls for something novel — a metaphor, a geographic rendering, a custom interactive — they build it from scratch with D3. This is why they use D3 instead of Chart.js or Highcharts: D3 gives them the freedom to invent.

4. TOOLS, NOT JUST GRAPHICS
The schools explorer, the redistricting tool, the legislative tracker, the election results pages — these aren't single-view graphics that get published and forgotten. They're products that readers use over weeks and months. The schools explorer has been running for years. The election results system gets rebuilt every election cycle. This requires a different engineering mindset: thinking about maintainability, data updates, edge cases, and long-term reliability.

5. SPEED ON BREAKING NEWS
The July 4 floods coverage, election night results — the team can ship quality work under extreme deadline pressure. This means having systems (like the election pipeline) that can be activated quickly, having team members who can make editorial and technical decisions fast, and having deployment workflows that don't bottleneck on approvals. Trevor's 15+ years in newsrooms means he already lives in this world.

6. DATA ANALYSIS AS JOURNALISM
Dan Keemahill's role as "data reporter" shows the team does the analysis, not just the visualization. They find the story in the data. Dan's Republican-shift-in-border-counties analysis wasn't assigned by an editor who already knew the trend — Dan found it by analyzing election data. This is investigative journalism applied to datasets instead of documents.
